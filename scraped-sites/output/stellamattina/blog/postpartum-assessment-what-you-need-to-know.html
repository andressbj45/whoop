Postpartum Assessment – What You Need to Know

June 20, 2024 ·By Dr. Theodore J. T. Krum

After the joy and exhaustion of childbirth, the postpartum period brings a new set of challenges and experiences for you as a new mother. Understanding the importance of having a postpartum assessment with your healthcare provider is crucial – not only for your health and recovery but also for your ability to care for your newborn effectively.

In this article, we’ll focus on what constitutes a thorough postpartum assessment.

Postpartum assessment involves evaluating your physical, emotional, and psychological well-being following the birth of your child. These regular assessments are crucial for identifying and addressing common postpartum issues to ensure you are

This broadly encompasses everything from physical recovery and mental health to addressing any complications that may have arisen during your pregnancy, labor, or delivery.

The two weeks postpartum appointment is typically scheduled within the first 14 days after delivery and focuses on immediate post-delivery concerns. This makes sense.

When you reach six weeks postpartum, a comprehensive follow-up appointment with your healthcare provider is crucial. This checkup serves as a fundamental step in ensuring that both your physical and emotional recovery are progressing well.

It’s vital to discuss any feelings of anxiety, depression, or general emotional distress you might be experiencing. If you’ve had mental health issues in the past, your provider might assess your risk for postnatal depression and anxiety more closely.

They may also ask about your lifestyle, including your support system at home and quality of sleep, to better understand your overall situation.

Your provider will check on your physical recovery from childbirth. This includes asking about vaginal bleeding and whether you’ve had a menstrual period since giving birth. If you experienced an episiotomy, the healing of your stitches will be assessed. Additionally, your doctor will address any discomfort around your perineum to ensure proper healing.

They will examine your abdomen to confirm that your uterus is returning to its pre-pregnancy position. If you had a cesarean section, your doctor will check how the incision is healing and may offer to examine the scar.

Although it might be the last thing on your mind, this appointment is an opportunity to discuss your plans for contraception and any questions you might have regarding resuming sexual activity.

Questions about urinary incontinence, such as leaking urine when coughing or laughing, are common. Your doctor will likely suggest pelvic floor exercises if you’re experiencing these issues.

Additionally, if you find yourself having accidental bowel movements or passing gas involuntarily, it’s important to bring this up during the checkup, as these can also be common postpartum concerns. Don’t be afraid to mention these things: Doctors who specialize in women’s health have heard it all before and will be empathic and sensitive.

Your healthcare provider should ask you about how breastfeeding is going – if you’re taking that route – and check for any issues such as pain or symptoms of mastitis. They will have access to resources for additional support if needed.

Postpartum labs are an integral part of the postpartum assessment. These might include blood tests to check hemoglobin levels, thyroid function tests, and screenings for postpartum depression.

If you notice any signs of varicose veins, leg swelling, or symptoms suggesting a blood clot, you should report them. Your healthcare provider may examine your legs to rule out any complications.

This is an opportunity to ensure vaccinations are up to date and schedule any necessary routine tests postponed due to pregnancy, such as smear tests.

Preparing for the postpartum period involves more than just mental preparation; it also requires practical items that can aid in recovery and comfort. Items such as high-absorbency pads, comfortable clothing, and pain relief medications are essential. Additionally, resources for breastfeeding, like nursing pads and creams, can help make the transition smoother.

Selecting a postpartum care service that aligns with your needs and preferences is critical. These services can range from in-home nurse visits to outpatient counseling sessions. They provide tailored support that can make a significant difference in your recovery and adaptation to life with your new baby.
