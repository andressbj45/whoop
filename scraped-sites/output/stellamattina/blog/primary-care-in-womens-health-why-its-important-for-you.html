Primary Care in Women’s Health: Why It’s Important for You

December 24, 2023 ·By Dr. Theodore J. T. Krum

Like many women, you’ve probably been tempted at times to put your own health second to everyone else’s! It’s what women so often do. But it’s not a good move long term. Women’s health matters for all sorts of reasons. And obviously, if you don’t look after yourself, you won’t be able to look after others.

We all make excuses for not attending primary care appointments. Do the following thoughts strike a chord?

“I’m really busy with work and school right now, and I feel healthy. I know I should probably get check-ups, but scheduling appointments and dealing with insurance is a hassle. I’ll think about it if I start feeling unwell.”

Or this one:

“I’ve been managing my own health for years, and I don’t like going to the doctor unless I have to. I’m concerned about having too many medications or unnecessary treatments. Besides, I’m more focused on taking care of my family.”

They both sound perfectly valid reasons for skipping a primary care appointment for your women’s annual checkup! But they’re actually self-sabotaging, because they can leave you possibly less healthy to do all that caring!

So, let’s take a closer look at the role of primary care in women’s health and move it up the agenda for all of us!

This isn’t a complete list. But to give you an idea of what a primary care physician does, or what you might be missing if you skip your annual visits, here are some common procedures and services:

Women’s health centers often provide special times when you can have a women’s wellness checkup or a women’s annual checkup. If you attend regularly for these, as well as when you have an acute need, your doctor will be able to keep track of your complete medical history.

The benefit is your doctor already understands you and your medical history when you attend for your next primary care visit. It’s joined-up work – in your favor.

So – let’s look at all the other benefits of primary care next.

Early detection of any health issue – such as the possibility of breast or cervical cancer – is crucial. If they’re caught and treated early, you will find it more than repays the time you spent making room for a primary care appointment!

If your women’s healthcare specialist in primary care knows you well from your regular yearly visits, they can do their assessment from a position of understanding you and your body. This is likely more accurate and useful. And if you want, for example, advice about losing weight or maintaining your target weight, they can help you with advice that’s geared to you and your body at any life stage.

Especially as you go through these life stages, regular health screenings can help in managing chronic conditions like hypertension, diabetes, and heart disease. These conditions can develop slowly and silently, so regular check-ups are vital in keeping them in check.

We mentioned earlier about building a relationship in terms of your primary care physician knowing your medical history, but it’s also about trust. When you know they’re focused on you and on women’s health, they become an ally you can trust to guide you in all your health decisions. You will feel free to be honest, and they will be able to provide care that’s aligned with your health goals. Perfect!

Within this environment of trust and care, you gain knowledge about your health. You may, for example, learn how important prenatal tests are for you and your baby. This knowledge, in turn, empowers you to make informed decisions about your health. Because there are always some decisions in women’s health that fall on you. Will you have this latest vaccination or not? Will you choose surgery or continue with pills?

Your primary care doctor is therefore at the heart of a strategy that supports your health as a woman throughout your life – from before your first period to the years after your menopause.

Of course, you may never need to use secondary specialist care. But if you do – for times like having a hysterectomy or a medical procedure – your primary care physician is who can connect and coordinate your care. As a specialist in women’s health, they’ll also be providing you with the services you need both before and after secondary care for example, prenatal care. It therefore makes sense to have a relationship with them already. They’ll understand your unique needs much better!
