Heart disease remains one of the leading causes of illness for adults in the U.S. The question of how we prevent heart disease matters if we want to live healthier lives, not just longer ones.

The good news is that many risk factors are modifiable.

Preventive care, everyday habits, and early awareness all play a role in helping you reduce your risk over time.

Understanding what heart disease is, how it develops, and what warning signs to watch for can make prevention feel more practical and achievable for you.

Heart Disease and Risk: The Basics

Heart disease is a broad term used to describe conditions that affect how your heart functions. Many heart-related problems develop gradually, influenced by your lifestyle, your genetics, and any underlying health conditions you have.

Preventive care from your Primary Care doctor focuses on identifying risk early and supporting your heart health before serious problems develop.

Heart Disease vs Congenital Heart Disease: What’s the Difference?

Heart disease and congenital heart disease are not the same.

Congenital heart disease refers to structural heart conditions present at birth.

Heart disease, on the other hand, typically develops later in life and is often linked to factors such as high blood pressure, high cholesterol, diabetes, smoking, and inactivity.

This distinction matters to you in the context of preventing heart disease because prevention strategies mainly apply to making sure you don’t acquire heart disease, not congenital conditions.

Is High Blood Pressure Considered Heart Disease?

High blood pressure is not classified as heart disease itself, but it is one of the strongest risk factors for developing it.

Over time, elevated blood pressure places strain on your heart and blood vessels, which increases the likelihood of heart-related complications developing.

But that’s something you can help prevent if you manage your blood pressure through lifestyle changes and regular checkups.

Why Is Physical Activity So Important in Preventing Heart Disease?

This is a key point. As a society, we’ve become used to sitting down a lot! It’s comfortable and our work often demands it.

But regular physical activity will support your heart health in multiple ways. For example, it:

  • Helps regulate blood pressure (see above for why it’s important)
  • Improves cholesterol levels (because raised LDL starts to block your arteries)
  • Supports blood sugar balance (muscle movement assists insulin in its job)
  • Strengthens the heart muscle (by giving your heart rate a workout!)

Movement also contributes to maintaining a healthy weight for you and managing stress – both important factors for good cardiovascular health.

Physical activity doesn’t have to mean intense workouts.

Consistent walking, cycling, swimming, or other moderate activities can make a meaningful difference when done regularly. You’re aiming for sustainability, not perfection.

Signs of Heart Disease You Shouldn’t Ignore

Recognizing early warning signs can lead to earlier evaluation and care so you stay healthy.

Common signs of heart disease may include chest discomfort, shortness of breath, palpitations, or unexplained fatigue. Symptoms can appear gradually and may not always feel dramatic, which is why people sometimes overlook them.

Signs of Women’s Heart Disease – and What About Men?

Both men and women can experience classic heart-related symptoms, such as chest pressure or pain. However, women are more likely to also experience less typical symptoms, including nausea, unusual fatigue, dizziness, or pain in the jaw, neck, or back.

Men may be more likely to report chest pain as a primary symptom, while women may notice subtler changes.

These differences don’t mean heart disease looks entirely different in women and men, but they do highlight why paying attention to any persistent or unusual symptoms matters – regardless of gender.

Everyday Ways to Prevent Heart Disease

Daily habits play a significant role in helping prevent heart disease. If you can make small, consistent changes, you’ll probably have more chance of staying healthy than trying for drastic changes that you then forget to keep up with.

So what preventive strategies do we advise at Stella Mattina?

1 Try to stay physically active on and off all day. If you work at a desk, get up and move every hour.

2 Eat a balanced diet choosing whole foods over ultra-processed ones where possible. These help your gut stay healthy, which can support weight loss or weight management.

3 Manage your stress levels. Meditation, coloring, calm breathing – anything that helps reset your system to normal.

4 Avoid smoking and, as much as possible, avoid inhaling smoke. These are major causes of heart problems.

5 Keep up with routine medical visits. Preventive screenings, such as blood pressure and cholesterol checks, help identify risk early.

February Is Heart Month: Why Awareness Matters

February is Heart Month, a time dedicated to raising awareness about heart health and prevention. It serves as a reminder for us all to schedule checkups, start or revisit healthy habits, and have conversations about family history and risk factors.

The aim of Heart Awareness Month is to emphasize the value of prevention, education, and early action rather than wait for symptoms to appear.

Some women’s pregnancies are already high risk because of existing health conditions. Others move from a low-risk to a high-risk category as new issues appear. Changes in symptoms, test results, or how your baby develops can shift how doctors classify your maternal fetal medicine care needs.

What Makes a Pregnancy High Risk

A pregnancy may be considered high risk when certain maternal or fetal factors increase the likelihood of medical concerns. These factors don’t guarantee problems, but they do raise pregnancy risks that benefit from closer medical oversight. Many high-risk pregnancy situations are common and manageable when identified early and followed carefully.

From a maternal perspective, chronic conditions such as high blood pressure, diabetes, heart disease, blood clotting disorders, and autoimmune disorders can significantly affect pregnancy.

In some cases, changes related to being pregnant, rather than the condition itself, raise concern and require closer observation.

Fetal factors also play an important role in determining whether a pregnancy is high risk. These include fetal anomalies, concerns about baby’s growth, or carrying twins or higher order multiples.

Each situation presents different pregnancy risks, and MFM specialists adjust care plans based on how your baby develops over time.

Importantly, high risk doesn’t describe a single moment or diagnosis. Risk can increase or decrease throughout pregnancy. And many women move along this spectrum as new information becomes available.

Understanding what places a pregnancy into a high-risk category helps doctors anticipate complications and tailor care to protect both you and your baby.

Understanding Your Health Risk Factors During Pregnancy

Health risk factors help explain why some pregnancies need closer attention than others. Many health conditions are manageable on their own. But they can become more complex when combined with the physical demands of pregnancy.

Chronic issues such as diabetes, autoimmune disorders, or other ongoing medical condition concerns may increase risk if they’re not carefully managed throughout pregnancy.

In addition, family history of certain conditions or previous pregnancy outcomes can influence how doctors assess current risk pregnancy and future pregnancies.

Lifestyle factors, including nutrition, activity level, and access to ongoing medical support, may also affect pregnancy in ways that are not always obvious early on.

These overlapping factors for pregnant women help explain why care plans need to be individualized and proactive to reduce complications.

They allow doctors to adjust your treatment and care for high-risk needs, and respond quickly. Effective care from a maternal fetal medicine specialist protects your health, your baby, and your overall well-being as pregnancy progresses.

But don’t assume you’ll have problems! Providers have a clear roadmap for managing risk and reducing uncertainty throughout your pregnancy.

Fetal Conditions That Require Specialized Monitoring

Some pregnancies require closer observation because of how your baby is developing. These conditions increase risk and need more frequent evaluation. Doctors can then respond early before complications arise.

For example, specialized ultrasound may show fetal anomalies involving your baby’s heart, brain, or other organs.

Your OBGYN or maternal fetal medicine specialist may have concerns about your baby’s growth, such as growth restriction or patterns that suggest your baby may not be receiving enough oxygen or nutrients.

Tracking these changes over time helps an MFM specialist determine how to adjust your pregnancy care.

Placental conditions are another important factor.

Issues such as placenta accreta (abnormal attachment of the placenta) can significantly increase risk, particularly later in pregnancy and during birth. Placenta accreta requires close monitoring and careful planning. In some high-risk situations, identifying placenta accreta early allows doctors to coordinate specialized care and reduce the chance of serious complications.

By combining detailed imaging with regular follow-up, care teams can better understand how fetal conditions may affect the course of pregnancy, keeping both you and your baby as safe as possible.

What is a Maternal Fetal Medicine Specialist?

A maternal fetal medicine specialist is an obstetrician with advanced training in managing complex high-risk pregnancies, including conditions that affect both mother and baby. The MFM has completed 4 years of OBGYN residency, followed by a 3-year MFM Fellowship program.

Their focus is on anticipating challenges, reducing risk, and supporting your safest possible path through pregnancy and birth.

Doctors trained in maternal fetal medicine work closely with your primary OBGYN and other specialists to provide coordinated, expert oversight.

In many cases, care involves these multiple doctors because they contribute different expertise, from cardiology to endocrinology, depending on the underlying medical condition. This collaborative care team approach ensures you get informed, timely decisions centered on both your health and your baby’s needs.

When Are Women Referred to a Maternal Fetal Medicine Specialist?

A maternal fetal medicine specialist referral happens when concerns such as high blood pressure, diabetes, placental disorders, or prior pregnancy complications raise concerns.

Referral doesn’t replace your OBGYN. Maternal fetal medicine adds another layer of insight and advanced care, especially for evolving risk pregnancy scenarios.

Guidelines from organizations such as the American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists support this team-based model, which is also reflected in academic centers like Weill Cornell Medicine.

Bringing together experienced doctors, highly trained specialists, and a coordinated team helps maternal fetal medicine deliver expert care for women who need care for high-risk situations throughout pregnancy.

Advanced Testing and Diagnostic Imaging Explained

Advanced testing helps doctors understand how your pregnancy is progressing and whether emerging concerns need intervention. In high-risk situations, routine screening may not provide enough detail. Which is why advanced testing and advanced imaging play an important role in guiding care decisions.

High-resolution ultrasound allows doctors to evaluate structure, movement, and circulation in greater detail. When an ultrasound shows changes in anatomy or development, providers can assess how those findings may affect your baby’s growth and overall health.

They may use additional imaging to monitor blood flow, placental function, or how well your baby is tolerating the pregnancy.

Laboratory evaluation also supports clinical decision-making.

A blood test may be used to assess infection risk, clotting concerns, or how your body is responding to conditions such as diabetes – or acquiring gestational diabetes. Monitoring blood sugar trends helps doctors treat complications and reduce risk to you and your baby.

Together, imaging and testing allow doctors to identify pregnancy concerns early and refine your care plans.

More About Genetic Conditions and Pregnancy Risk

Some pregnancies involve an increased level of risk because of inherited or chromosomal factors.

Genetic conditions may be known before conception or identified during pregnancy through screening and diagnostic evaluation.

In other cases, a genetic disorder is suspected later in pregnancy based on imaging findings, lab results, or a known family history.

These situations can feel overwhelming. But clear information helps guide next steps.

When genetic concerns are identified, genetic counselors play an important role in helping patients understand what the findings mean. Genetic counseling explains:

  • How specific genetic conditions may affect pregnancy
  • The likelihood of recurrence in future pregnancies
  • What monitoring or planning may be helpful

This supports informed decision-making without assuming a single outcome for your baby.

Advances in screening and diagnostic tools also support proactive care. In some cases, testing is part of preconception counseling. In others, it becomes relevant later in pregnancy as new information emerges.

Understanding genetic risk factors allows doctors to tailor monitoring, coordinate appropriate treatment, and plan your delivery or specialist newborn care.

With the right support, most women navigate genetic concerns while maintaining focus on their baby, their health, and the safest possible course through pregnancy.

How to lose weight with PCOS (the usual way we refer to polycystic ovarian syndrome) is often challenging, but it’s definitely not impossible.

If you have a PCOS diagnosis, you’ve come to the right place to learn about how to lose weight with PCOS.

But first we need to briefly clarify two things:

The first is that you can have multiple ovarian cysts but not have PCOS. There’s a difference. And as we mentioned in our blog on ovarian cysts and birth control pills, they may cause bloating but not usually weight gain – although it can feel like it.

The second is that multiple ovarian cysts might be linked to PCOS sometimes. A polycystic ovarian syndrome specialist will be able to tell you how PCOS is diagnosed, how to get tested for PCOS, and offer a specific PCOS test – such as the PCOS ultrasound test.

But here, we’re going to look at how to lose weight with PCOS. Why is it so hard?

Why Weight Loss With PCOS Can Be Hard

Weight loss with PCOS is affected by hormonal and metabolic factors that don’t apply in the same way to everyone else.

One of the most significant is insulin resistance, which is common in PCOS. When insulin levels are consistently high, the body is more likely to store fat and less likely to access stored fat for energy.

PCOS can also affect how your body regulates appetite and energy use.

Hormonal imbalances may increase hunger signals, reduce satiety after meals, and make blood sugar swings more pronounced.

This is why standard calorie-cutting approaches often lead to short-term results followed by plateaus or rapid regain.

Another issue is this: In PCOS, your body may burn fewer calories at rest than expected, particularly during aggressive dieting. That’s why restrictive diets are often ineffective over time if you have PCOS.

None of this means weight loss with PCOS isn’t achievable. You just need an effective strategy. One that reduces insulin resistance and stress levels over time.

How to Lose Weight With PCOS: Targeting Polycystic Ovarian Syndrome Belly

You may have noticed that your extra weight gain is mostly around your abdomen. This pattern is closely linked to insulin resistance and cortisol (often referred to as the stress hormone).

When insulin levels remain elevated, fat storage is more likely to occur centrally than in your hips or thighs. Stress hormones can amplify this effect. Particularly if your sleep is poor or exercise intensity is too high for recovery.

This pattern can be frustrating.

But belly fat is responsive if you can improve your insulin sensitivity. For example, with resistance training, balanced meals with adequate protein and fiber, and consistent meal timing.

Targeting abdominal fat by addressing blood sugar regulation, stress load, and muscle preservation is what will bring you gradual but measurable change.

What Is the PCOS Diet?

There isn’t a single prescribed PCOS diet. But there are clear nutritional patterns that support PCOS weight loss by improving blood sugar control and reducing insulin spikes. You’re aiming for stability.

Foods That Support PCOS Weight Loss

Meals that help you lose weight with PCOS tend to combine fiber, protein, and healthy fats. This slows digestion, reduces blood sugar swings, and helps control your appetite across the day. It also helps your gut health.

Fiber-rich carbohydrates are a key part of this in appropriate portions.

Whole grains such as oatmeal, brown rice, and quinoa, along with beans, lentils, vegetables, and fruit, help regulate insulin response.

Protein is equally important.

Include protein at every meal – from foods such as fish, eggs, poultry, legumes, tofu, or Greek yogurt. This supports muscle mass and helps you feel full for longer.

Maintaining muscle is particularly important for PCOS weight loss. Why? Because muscle tissue improves insulin sensitivity.

Healthy fats also play a role.

Foods such as olive oil, nuts, seeds, and avocado support hormone function and slow absorption of carbs when eaten together in a meal.

Overall, diets that are plant-forward, minimally processed, and balanced tend to be easier to maintain and more effective for PCOS weight loss than highly restrictive plans.

You can start swapping these foods in gradually to make the change easier. And also swap some foods out…

Foods to Limit if You’re Trying to Lose Weight With PCOS

Foods that cause rapid blood sugar spikes can make weight loss with PCOS more difficult. These include refined carbs, sugary drinks, sweets, and heavily processed snack foods.

Highly processed foods are often low in fiber and high in added sugars or refined starches. This can increase hunger shortly after eating.

Regularly relying on these foods can undermine your weight-loss efforts even when you’ve monitored calories.

Limiting these foods doesn’t mean complete avoidance. The focus is frequency and portion size, not perfection.

Meal Timing and Balance Helps PCOS Weight Loss

Skipping meals or eating erratically can worsen blood sugar instability. Regular meals with a balance of carbohydrates, protein, and fat tend to support more consistent energy levels and appetite control.

What Else Helps You Lose Weight With PCOS?

Food choices form the foundation of PCOS weight loss, but lifestyle factors influence how effectively those changes work. Here are two to bear in mind:

Exercise Can Support PCOS Weight Loss

Exercise is most effective for PCOS when it improves insulin sensitivity rather than simply burning calories.

Resistance training plays a central role here. Building and maintaining muscle helps your body use glucose more efficiently and supports metabolic health over time.

Consistent low-impact cardio such as walking, cycling, or swimming can also be helpful. These support fat loss without placing excessive stress on your body.

However, very high volumes of intense cardio are not always helpful for PCOS. In some cases, they can increase stress hormones and make appetite and fatigue harder to manage.

Sleep and Stress Management

Poor sleep and chronic stress can interfere with PCOS weight loss by increasing cortisol levels and disrupting appetite regulation.

Even small improvements in sleep duration or consistency can support better blood sugar control and reduce your cravings.

And finding ways to manage your stress levels can help support your weight loss efforts.

Ovarian cysts most often develop as part of normal hormone changes during the menstrual cycle, particularly around ovulation. Birth control pills can influence whether some cysts form by changing how the ovaries function, but their role depends on the type of cyst and the person’s symptoms.

Understanding what’s typical, what’s not, and when treatment is needed will help you clarify how ovarian cysts and birth control are related.

Let’s start with what exactly they are.

Understanding Ovarian Cysts

Cysts of this kind are fluid-filled sacs that form on or inside your ovary, and they’re very common during the reproductive years.

In many cases, they develop naturally as your ovaries prepare to release an egg – and resolve without causing symptoms.

Because cysts can form for different reasons and behave in different ways, doctors look at timing, size, and symptoms before deciding whether you need any follow-up or treatment.

What Causes Ovarian Cysts?

Most ovarian cysts form as part of the normal ovulation process.

Each month, your ovaries prepare and release an egg. During that cycle, a small sac can develop and sometimes continue to fill with fluid instead of breaking down as expected.

Hormonal shifts, timing within your menstrual cycle, and how your ovary responds to those hormones all play a role in what causes ovarian cysts to form in the first place.

However, not all cysts are related to ovulation. Some develop independently of your menstrual cycle and behave differently over time.

This is why clinicians focus on how a cyst looks, how long it’s been present, and whether symptoms are changing, rather than assuming all cysts follow the same pattern.

How Birth Control Changes the Picture

The relationship between ovarian cysts and the pill comes down to ovulation.

Combined hormonal birth control pills work by preventing ovulation, which reduces the chance of forming the most common type of functional cyst.

This means the pill can lower the likelihood of new cysts developing – rather than acting as a treatment for cysts that already exist.

Because not all ovarian cysts are caused by ovulation, however, birth control does not affect every cyst in the same way.

Whether the pill is helpful depends on the type of cyst, your symptom history, and your overall reproductive health. That’s why treatment decisions are individualized.

Symptoms to Watch For

You may not have any symptoms at all. Many ovarian cysts only come to light during routine exams or imaging done for another reason. But for other people, symptoms can vary widely.

They’re often related to pressure or irritation in the pelvis rather than the cyst itself being dangerous.

Common cyst-on-ovary symptoms include pelvic discomfort, bloating, a feeling of fullness, pain on one side of the lower abdomen, or discomfort during sex.

Changes in symptoms matter more than their presence alone.

So, take note of worsening pain, new sensations that don’t follow your usual cycle pattern, or symptoms that interfere with daily life.

These are typically what prompt further evaluation, rather than mild or occasional discomfort on its own.

When an Ovarian Cyst Becomes Urgent

While most ovarian cysts are harmless, some situations need prompt medical attention. An ovarian cyst rupture can cause sudden, sharp pelvic pain, sometimes accompanied by nausea, vomiting, dizziness, or fever.

This kind of pain is typically different from the dull or intermittent discomfort many people notice with uncomplicated cysts.

Size can also matter, but it isn’t the only factor doctors consider.

There’s no single cutoff that defines what size of ovarian cyst is dangerous. Symptoms, appearance, and how quickly a cyst changes are just as important as measurements.

For example, a smaller cyst causing severe pain may be more concerning than a larger one that remains stable and symptom-free.

How Doctors Diagnose Ovarian Cysts

The most common diagnosing tool is imaging, particularly an ultrasound on ovarian cysts. This test allows doctors to see the cyst’s size, shape, and internal features – which helps distinguish between cyst types and guides next steps.

Doctors can also use this non-invasive ultrasound of ovaries for initial diagnosis and follow-up to see whether a cyst resolves on its own.

They interpret imaging results alongside your symptoms, medical history, and where you are in your menstrual cycle.

This combination helps determine whether monitoring is best or whether treatment might be appropriate.

Ovarian Cyst Treatment Options

Ovarian cyst treatment depends on several factors, including symptoms, cyst appearance on imaging, age, and whether the cyst changes over time.

In many cases, you don’t need immediate treatment. Functional cysts often resolve on their own, and follow-up imaging can confirm if they’re shrinking or disappearing.

When symptoms are present, treatment may focus on pain management and monitoring rather than intervention.

Surgery is typically reserved for cysts that persist, grow, cause significant symptoms, or appear suspicious on imaging.

The goal is always to match the level of treatment to your level of risk, rather than treating every cyst the same way.

Birth Control Pills and Cyst Prevention

Birth control pills for ovarian cysts are most often used to help prevent new functional cysts from forming, not to treat existing ones.

By suppressing ovulation, hormonal birth control reduces the chance that the ovary will form the type of cyst most commonly linked to the menstrual cycle.

This approach isn’t necessary or helpful for everyone.

Because some ovarian cysts are not related to ovulation, doctors usually base decisions about using hormonal contraception on symptom patterns, recurrence, and your overall reproductive health, rather than the presence of a single cyst.

When to See an OBGYN

It’s a good idea to talk with a doctor if ovarian cyst symptoms persist, worsen, or keep returning over time.

Ongoing pelvic pain, changes in your menstrual cycle, pain during sex, or symptoms that interfere with daily life are all reasons to seek care rather than wait things out. Even when cysts are common and often harmless, patterns over time matter.

An OBGYN can help determine whether monitoring is enough or whether further evaluation makes sense – especially if imaging shows changes or symptoms don’t line up with a typical cycle.

How Hormonal Changes After Birth Affect Periods

After delivery, your body experiences some dramatic hormonal changes.

During pregnancy, hormone levels stay consistently high to support your baby. But once you give birth and the placenta is delivered, those hormone levels drop sharply.

What is Menopause and Why Does It Matter for Your Health?

Menopause is a natural biological process that marks the end of your reproductive years. It happens when your ovaries slow their hormone production, especially estrogen and progesterone, and your menstrual periods stop for good.

These hormonal shifts aren’t sudden. Your hormone levels fluctuate for years before your final period. Which is why so many physical and emotional changes appear gradually.

Most women reach natural menopause between ages 45 and 55, although genetics, health conditions, surgeries, and lifestyle factors can all influence timing.

But some women experience early menopause, while others notice changes later. Wherever you fall, this transition affects far more than your reproductive years!

Menopause is a meaningful phase in women’s health because changing hormones influence long-term wellbeing. Bone strength, cardiovascular health, metabolic balance, sexual comfort, and mental wellness all respond to these shifts.

If you don’t manage your symptoms, they can contribute to issues like osteoporosis or increased heart disease risk over time.

Understanding how the menopause transition works – and knowing what’s normal for each stage – helps you manage symptoms, stay active, and feel more in control of the changes ahead.

How to Know You’re Going Through Menopause

Most women don’t wake up one morning and think, “It’s starting.” You may only recognize the menopause transition when a few changes stack up over time.

Shifts in your menstrual periods are often the earliest clue. Cycles may become shorter, longer, lighter, or heavier – i.e. unpredictable.

But you might notice earlier symptoms like sleep disruption, mood swings, hot flashes, or new sensitivity to stress. Some women experience subtle cognitive shifts, like trouble concentrating. Or they begin to experience menopausal symptoms that don’t seem connected at first.

Menopause becomes “official” when you’ve gone 12 straight months without a period. This marks the moment your ovaries have slowed their hormone output enough that hormonal changes stabilize into a new pattern.

Pre-menopause is the stage when your hormones still follow their regular rhythm. No noticeable menopausal symptoms. Your periods arrive on time.

Perimenopause is where most women begin to feel change. Hormone levels rise and fall unevenly, so symptoms can be unpredictable. Hot flashes, sleep disruption, mood changes, and shifts in bleeding patterns show up. This stage can last several years, and symptoms tend to ebb and flow.

Menopause is a single point in time: the day you reach 12 months without a period. You may still have symptoms, but hormonally, it marks your body settling into a more consistent pattern. You move through the menopause transition at your own pace. The stages don’t follow a strict schedule, yet research and clinical experience give us a general guide.

Pre-menopause lasts from your teens through your 30s and early 40s. Hormones stay fairly steady, your menstrual periods follow your usual rhythm, and menopause symptoms haven’t started yet.

Perimenopause is the most variable stage. It can last two or three years or closer to a decade. Fluctuating hormone levels cause symptoms to come and go. Menopause itself is only a moment in time, as we said. But the symptoms surrounding it may continue for several years.

These mood swings, anxiety, irritability, or feeling less emotionally steady aren’t “in your head.” They’re tied to hormonal changes affecting neurotransmitters that regulate stress and emotional balance.

Sexual desire may change during the menopause transition, and the combination of low estrogen, dryness, and reduced sleep can make intimacy unwelcome.

Rest and Recovery

Sleep can become more difficult during perimenopause and post menopause. Build a wind-down routine, adjust your sleep environment, and keep consistent sleep times to reduce stress and help reduce hot flashes and mood swings.

Mental Health

Shifts in hormones can influence emotional steadiness. Any stress-reduction practices can help make the transition more manageable. Good midlife women’s healthcare includes having someone to talk to during menopause transitions!

Connection
Menopause is easier if you connect with friends, family, support groups, or your healthcare providers. Evidence-based group resources from the Menopause Society or local women’s health networks can help you stay informed, feel supported, and know which questions to bring to a menopause specialist.

Together, these pillars help you feel more in control of the transition.

Understanding Acute and Chronic Conditions

Acute issues come on quickly, change quickly, and usually resolve within days or weeks.

Chronic conditions last longer, usually defined as longer than a year, and often for life. They need ongoing management so they don’t interfere with your daily routine.

You may also hear doctors talk about “acute chronic conditions.” This is when something short-term flares up because of something long-term in the background. For example, a sudden asthma attack when you live with asthma, or high blood pressure spiking during a stressful week.

Knowing the difference between acute and chronic conditions helps you understand what’s happening in your body and when to reach out for care.

Acute Conditions Show Up in Everyday Life

Acute conditions often make themselves known right away. Examples include catching a common cold, or coming down with flu, a strep throat, or falling and breaking your wrist. These changes tend to be noticeable, disruptive, and new.

They usually have a clear cause: an infection, an injury, or exposure to someone else who’s sick.

Most acute conditions settle with rest, hydration, or simple medicines. One example is having sneezes and congestion during the peak allergy season.

But some deserve quicker medical care – like a high fever that won’t ease, a cough that gets worse instead of better, or an injury that makes it hard to move.

Primary care clinics handle acute appointments every day. If something feels off or painful and you want clarity, they can usually see you quickly, give you a diagnosis, and start the right treatment plan.

That’s acute illness. So how is chronic illness different?

Chronic Conditions Affect Your Long-Term Health

Chronic conditions stay with you over the long haul. They might be quiet and manageable for months at a time, then shift when stress rises or life gets busy.

What is a Clinical Breast Exam?

Screening tests for breast cancer involve a thorough check of your breast health. During a breast cancer check-up, or CBE, your OBGYN will carry out three checks:

1 Take a health history

They’ll ask you about any symptoms you’ve noticed, your medical history, or a family history of breast problems.

Symptoms might include any tenderness or a leakage from a nipple. But mostly, regular breast cancer check-ups happen before you notice any physical symptoms.

You can help them in this part of the exam by taking along a note of information you want to remember. Especially about your health history or family experience of breast cancer.

Your gynecologist is checking for any changes in size or shape, puckers, dimples, redness, or rashes.

3 Manually examine your breasts

This is the part some women feel a little self-conscious about. But your OBGYN is expert and professional when manually checking for any lumps, thickening, or changes in each breast.

Like we said, this is a routine you can start at home. You might want to pin a reminder in your calendar. Regular checks mean you can find any possible problems before they become untreatable.

What Happens Next If You Have an Abnormal Finding?

Most abnormal findings are benign (not cancer). But follow-up is essential for a definitive diagnosis.

If your OBGYN finds something suspicious, they’ll likely refer you to a specialist breast clinic for a triple assessment, which can take several hours in one visit:

  1. Clinical breast examination: A specialist doctor or nurse will perform another physical exam.
  2. Imaging tests:
    • Diagnostic mammogram: This is a more detailed low-dose breast X-ray of the specific area of concern.
    • Breast ultrasound: This test uses sound waves to determine if a lump is a fluid-filled cyst (usually benign) or a solid mass (which might need further testing).
    • Breast MRI: This exam may be used in some cases, particularly if you’re a high-risk woman or if findings are unclear on other imaging.

Surgery removes as much of the cancer as possible, helping prevent it from spreading.

Chemotherapy targets cancer cells throughout the breasts and lymph nodes to eliminate them.

Radiation therapy aims to destroy any remaining cancer cells in a specific area.
