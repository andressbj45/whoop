Heart disease remains one of the leading causes of illness for adults in the U.S. The question of how we prevent heart disease matters if we want to live healthier lives, not just longer ones.

The good news is that many risk factors are modifiable.

Preventive care, everyday habits, and early awareness all play a role in helping you reduce your risk over time.

Understanding what heart disease is, how it develops, and what warning signs to watch for can make prevention feel more practical and achievable for you.

Heart Disease and Risk: The Basics

Heart disease is a broad term used to describe conditions that affect how your heart functions. Many heart-related problems develop gradually, influenced by your lifestyle, your genetics, and any underlying health conditions you have.

Preventive care from your Primary Care doctor focuses on identifying risk early and supporting your heart health before serious problems develop.

Heart Disease vs Congenital Heart Disease: What’s the Difference?

Heart disease and congenital heart disease are not the same.

Congenital heart disease refers to structural heart conditions present at birth.

Heart disease, on the other hand, typically develops later in life and is often linked to factors such as high blood pressure, high cholesterol, diabetes, smoking, and inactivity.

This distinction matters to you in the context of preventing heart disease because prevention strategies mainly apply to making sure you don’t acquire heart disease, not congenital conditions.

Is High Blood Pressure Considered Heart Disease?

High blood pressure is not classified as heart disease itself, but it is one of the strongest risk factors for developing it.

Over time, elevated blood pressure places strain on your heart and blood vessels, which increases the likelihood of heart-related complications developing.

But that’s something you can help prevent if you manage your blood pressure through lifestyle changes and regular checkups.

Why Is Physical Activity So Important in Preventing Heart Disease?

This is a key point. As a society, we’ve become used to sitting down a lot! It’s comfortable and our work often demands it.

But regular physical activity will support your heart health in multiple ways. For example, it:

  • Helps regulate blood pressure (see above for why it’s important)
  • Improves cholesterol levels (because raised LDL starts to block your arteries)
  • Supports blood sugar balance (muscle movement assists insulin in its job)
  • Strengthens the heart muscle (by giving your heart rate a workout!)

Movement also contributes to maintaining a healthy weight for you and managing stress – both important factors for good cardiovascular health.

Physical activity doesn’t have to mean intense workouts.

Consistent walking, cycling, swimming, or other moderate activities can make a meaningful difference when done regularly. You’re aiming for sustainability, not perfection.

Signs of Heart Disease You Shouldn’t Ignore

Recognizing early warning signs can lead to earlier evaluation and care so you stay healthy.

Common signs of heart disease may include chest discomfort, shortness of breath, palpitations, or unexplained fatigue. Symptoms can appear gradually and may not always feel dramatic, which is why people sometimes overlook them.

Signs of Women’s Heart Disease – and What About Men?

Both men and women can experience classic heart-related symptoms, such as chest pressure or pain. However, women are more likely to also experience less typical symptoms, including nausea, unusual fatigue, dizziness, or pain in the jaw, neck, or back.

Men may be more likely to report chest pain as a primary symptom, while women may notice subtler changes.

These differences don’t mean heart disease looks entirely different in women and men, but they do highlight why paying attention to any persistent or unusual symptoms matters – regardless of gender.

Everyday Ways to Prevent Heart Disease

Daily habits play a significant role in helping prevent heart disease. If you can make small, consistent changes, you’ll probably have more chance of staying healthy than trying for drastic changes that you then forget to keep up with.

So what preventive strategies do we advise at Stella Mattina?

1 Try to stay physically active on and off all day. If you work at a desk, get up and move every hour.

2 Eat a balanced diet choosing whole foods over ultra-processed ones where possible. These help your gut stay healthy, which can support weight loss or weight management.

3 Manage your stress levels. Meditation, coloring, calm breathing – anything that helps reset your system to normal.

4 Avoid smoking and, as much as possible, avoid inhaling smoke. These are major causes of heart problems.

5 Keep up with routine medical visits. Preventive screenings, such as blood pressure and cholesterol checks, help identify risk early.

February Is Heart Month: Why Awareness Matters

February is Heart Month, a time dedicated to raising awareness about heart health and prevention. It serves as a reminder for us all to schedule checkups, start or revisit healthy habits, and have conversations about family history and risk factors.

The aim of Heart Awareness Month is to emphasize the value of prevention, education, and early action rather than wait for symptoms to appear.

High cholesterol is one of those health issues that often develops quietly. You may feel perfectly well, yet changes inside your blood vessels can already be underway.

That’s why preventive care plays such an important role in living healthily for longer. Not just for older adults, but for younger families with children and teens too.

But let’s start with understanding exactly what cholesterol refers to.

Understanding Cholesterol

Cholesterol is a waxy substance found in your blood. Your body needs some cholesterol to function, but too much of certain types can increase the risk of heart disease over time.

A standard blood panel looks at different components, including LDL (“bad” cholesterol), HDL (“good” cholesterol), and triglycerides. Your primary care provider helps interpret these numbers in context – not in isolation. That means taking into account your age, your medical history, and your overall health.

Persistently high levels of LDL cholesterol can contribute to the gradual buildup of fatty material inside your arteries.

Over time, this can interfere with healthy blood flow and increase the likelihood of serious heart-related complications. How this risk develops can vary between women and men, which we’ll explore shortly.

But first, people often ask us, is high cholesterol inherited?

Is High Cholesterol Genetic?

Public messaging often focuses heavily on diet, which can make it surprising to learn that cholesterol levels are not determined by food alone.

For some individuals, genetics plays a significant role. If close family members have a history of elevated cholesterol or early heart disease, your own risk may be higher.

This is one reason doctors for high cholesterol often recommend earlier or more regular screening, even for people who otherwise feel well.

Symptoms of Raised Cholesterol in Females

The warning signs people usually hear about are not symptoms of raised cholesterol itself, but of cardiovascular disease that can develop after cholesterol has been elevated for some time. These include:

  • Chest pain
  • Shortness of breath
  • Unexplained fatigue (more on this later)
  • A high blood pressure reading
  • Evidence of poor circulation

Hormonal shifts during pregnancy, perimenopause, and menopause may have influenced your cholesterol levels without causing any of these symptoms – even though your lifestyle habits remain the same.

So, the first you may know about high cholesterol levels as a woman can be a heart attack. This is partly because women may develop cardiovascular disease differently, with changes in smaller blood vessels and less obvious early blockage.

In men, the pattern is often different.

What Men Should Know About Cholesterol Risk

Men may develop cholesterol-related risks earlier in adulthood, particularly when combined with factors such as family history or limited physical activity. Like women, they often feel well even when levels are elevated.

But when cholesterol really builds up in their arteries, they often get the symptoms we mentioned – due to the fatty blockage.

So, men often present acutely rather than via routine primary care. You’ll likely have heard about men needing stents after presenting with symptoms caused by significant arterial narrowing.

In many cases, underlying cholesterol issues surface at that point. And the diagnosis of seriously raised cholesterol leads to treatment.

Why You Often Miss Symptoms

As we said, one of the challenges with cholesterol in the earlier stages is that it rarely causes obvious symptoms. You may only discover elevated levels after a routine screening.

Some people ask us whether fatigue can be related. Can high cholesterol cause fatigue?

On its own, no. Cholesterol doesn’t usually create noticeable tiredness. However, raised cholesterol often exists alongside other health factors – such as blood pressure changes or metabolic issues. And these may affect your energy levels.

This is another reason testing matters more than waiting for symptoms.

Cholesterol Screening for Children and Teens

Cholesterol isn’t only an adult issue. In some cases, early screening is recommended for children and adolescents, especially when there’s a strong family history of cholesterol or heart disease.

In addition, rising rates of childhood overweight mean it’s increasingly important to consider cholesterol risk early to protect their future health.

Your primary care doctor can advise when testing makes sense and how they interpret results at younger ages.

The goal is not to label children, but to guide healthy development and reduce future risk in your family.

How Doctors Test For High Cholesterol

A test for high cholesterol is simple and widely available through our primary care clinics in Dallas and Carrollton. It usually involves a blood draw, sometimes after fasting, depending on what your doctor recommends.

A high cholesterol blood test provides a snapshot of current levels and helps establish a baseline for future comparison. Over time, trends often matter more than a single number.

That’s because primary care doctors support cholesterol management by looking at the whole person, not just lab results.

This includes discussing lifestyle factors, monitoring changes over time, and determining when additional support or referrals may be appropriate.

Acute and chronic conditions affect your health in very different ways and have different timelines. Understanding how they work helps you know when to book an appointment and what kind of care you might need.

So, if you’ve ever wondered what is the difference between acute and chronic conditions, you’re in the right place.

Understanding Acute and Chronic Conditions

Acute issues come on quickly, change quickly, and usually resolve within days or weeks.

Chronic conditions last longer, usually defined as longer than a year, and often for life. They need ongoing management so they don’t interfere with your daily routine.

You may also hear doctors talk about “acute chronic conditions.” This is when something short-term flares up because of something long-term in the background. For example, a sudden asthma attack when you live with asthma, or high blood pressure spiking during a stressful week.

Knowing the difference between acute and chronic conditions helps you understand what’s happening in your body and when to reach out for care.

Acute Conditions Show Up in Everyday Life

Acute conditions often make themselves known right away. Examples include catching a common cold, or coming down with flu, a strep throat, or falling and breaking your wrist. These changes tend to be noticeable, disruptive, and new.

They usually have a clear cause: an infection, an injury, or exposure to someone else who’s sick.

Most acute conditions settle with rest, hydration, or simple medicines. One example is having sneezes and congestion during the peak allergy season.

But some deserve quicker medical care – like a high fever that won’t ease, a cough that gets worse instead of better, or an injury that makes it hard to move.

Primary care clinics handle acute appointments every day. If something feels off or painful and you want clarity, they can usually see you quickly, give you a diagnosis, and start the right treatment plan.

That’s acute illness. So how is chronic illness different?

Chronic Conditions Affect Your Long-Term Health

Chronic conditions stay with you over the long haul. They might be quiet and manageable for months at a time, then shift when stress rises or life gets busy.

The more consistent you are with attending to these details for chronic care, the fewer flare-ups you’ll face.

Does weight loss feel like a puzzle with missing pieces? You eat well, you stay active, but the results stall? The hidden puzzle piece is often something invisible: your hormones.

Think of these as your body’s chemical messengers – your built-in communication system. Hormones carry signals that control your metabolism, including hunger and mood, and also how efficiently your cells burn energy or store fat.

In other words, your hormones play a role in how you gain, lose, and maintain body weight.

When their messages are clear and balanced, your metabolism runs smoothly. When signals are messy, your metabolism slows and your body starts protecting its energy stores instead of releasing them.

That’s why you can keep the same routine, calories, and workouts as before and still see no progress. Hormonal imbalance can shift the entire equation – altering your appetite cues, slowing your energy burn, or encouraging fat storage.

When you know that hormones regulate metabolism, you understand why “eat less, move more” isn’t always enough!

In this article, we’ll look at how key hormones interact, what throws them off balance, and how restoring hormonal health can open the door to lasting weight loss.

What Are Weight Loss Hormones?

As mentioned, hormones are your body’s chemical messengers – tiny molecules that move through your bloodstream, delivering instructions from one organ to another.

Within your endocrine system (a.k.a. your hormone system), dozens of these messengers shape how you feel, function, and respond to daily habits.

A hormone produced in one part of your body can influence tissues far away, adjusting energy use, appetite, and mood in milliseconds.

When it comes to maintaining or changing your body composition (which is what you’re aiming to do), a handful of hormones carry particular influence.

They include insulin, cortisol, estrogen, leptin, ghrelin, thyroid hormones, and testosterone. Together these coordinate hunger, fat storage, and how efficiently your cells turn food into usable energy.

Why are these called weight loss hormones?

Because if hormone levels drift too high or too low, the conversation between them becomes distorted! You might feel hungry when you’re actually full, or sluggish when you’ve slept well.

These are early signs of hormone imbalance – signals that your body’s internal communication may need looking at.

Balanced hormones support healthy weight management because they align your body’s natural metabolic rhythms.

How Hormones Affect Fat Storage and Metabolism

Hormones determine how your body uses, stores, or releases energy.

Inside this chemical network of hormones, certain messengers decide when to burn fuel and when to hold on to it as body fat.

Insulin, for example, helps move glucose from the bloodstream into your cells for energy. But when your cells stop responding properly – a state known as insulin resistance – your body compensates by releasing more insulin to try to regulate blood sugar levels. But lack of insulin sensitivity encourages fat storage rather than fat burning.

Cortisol, your body’s main stress signal, works in a similar way. Chronically high cortisol levels raise blood sugar and cravings, particularly for refined carbohydrates, which then feed the insulin cycle again.

Can it get worse? Yes!

Thyroid hormones regulate your metabolic “speed.” Even a subtle decline can reduce how much daily energy you actually use up, leaving you tired and prone to storing body fat.

Estrogen and testosterone influence where that fat sits – often shifting toward your abdomen when hormonal imbalance sets in.

Leptin and ghrelin work to regulate your appetite (leptin) or stimulate hunger (ghrelin – the hunger hormone!). You need them to work well. If your leptin levels get out of sync, leptin resistance sets in, making it hard to lose weight.

Of course, external factors (such as stress, poor sleep, nutrient-poor diets, and age-related changes) can increase the effects of hormone imbalance.

When that happens, your metabolism becomes even less predictable and your appetite cues less reliable. It then becomes harder to lose weight even when your lifestyle habits seem sound!

Can hormones cause weight gain and fatigue? Yes, certain imbalances like thyroid dysfunction or insulin resistance can lead to both.

Recognizing how interconnected these hormone pathways are is the first step toward identifying which hormone might be holding your progress back.

Common Hormones That Influence Weight Gain and Loss

Let’s look more closely at four of the common weight loss hormones.

Insulin – the Fat-Storage Hormone

Insulin’s job is simple but powerful: it helps your body move glucose into cells for energy. When your diet is balanced and your insulin sensitivity is good, you use energy efficiently and your body fat stays stable.

But frequent spikes in insulin – often from ultra-processed foods or constant snacking – can cause cells to stop responding. That’s insulin resistance, and it’s your body’s signal to store rather than burn fat. So, it converts extra glucose into body fat, especially around your abdomen.

This process links directly to weight gain and, over time, to metabolic syndrome and fatigue. However, restoring insulin balance through medical guidance and a nutrient-dense diet can help your body relearn how to lose weight effectively.

Cortisol – the Stress Hormone

Cortisol is your built-in alarm system. The stress hormone rises when life feels dangerous. Short bursts of stress keep you alert. But when cortisol levels stay high, your body interprets stress as danger and clings to its energy reserves.

That means more food cravings, slower metabolism, and central weight gain. Elevated cortisol also raises insulin, again increasing fat storage.

So, can your hormones stop you from losing weight? Yes – when cortisol and insulin remain high together, fat-burning signals get silenced.

You can help reopen the pathway to healthy weight loss by managing stress and restoring healthy cortisol patterns.

Estrogen – the Female Hormone and the Weight Gain Connection

Estrogen helps regulate your mood, bone density, and how your body stores fat. When estrogen is balanced, it protects muscle tone and supports metabolic efficiency – which is what we’re looking for to help correct hormone imbalance.

Problems arise when estrogen levels swing too high or too low.

“Estrogen dominance” is often a result of stress, environmental toxins, or excess body fat. It can lead to stubborn weight gain, fluid retention, and mood shifts.

On the other hand, low estrogen (or estrogen deficiency) after menopause can make postmenopausal women more prone to belly fat and reduced energy use.

In both cases, these hormonal shifts in your endocrine system reduce calorie burn and can make it harder to lose weight, even with good habits.

Thyroid Hormones – the Metabolic Regulators

Your thyroid gland releases thyroid hormones that act like a thermostat for your metabolism. When levels fall, your metabolism slows, causing fatigue, dry skin, and weight gain that resists weight loss effort.

A mild decline in thyroid hormone output can reduce energy expenditure by hundreds of calories a day! That’s why even your healthy diet sometimes isn’t enough.

As part of your medical weight-loss plan in Dallas, we suggest lab testing for thyroid function. If we find an imbalance, we can target your treatment and help re-ignite energy production so your body can lose weight sustainably.

Signs You Have a Hormonal Imbalance

Hormones affect every system in your body, so when they fall out of sync, and diet and exercise no longer work for weight loss, the signs can appear almost anywhere.

A subtle hormonal imbalance can develop gradually, often after stress, pregnancy, or perimenopause. And many people overlook the hormone connection.

So – if you’re thinking, “how do I know if hormones are affecting my weight?” typical symptoms include:

  • Unexplained weight gain or sudden changes in body composition
  • Fatigue that lingers even after a good night’s sleep
  • Mood swings or brain fog
  • Irregular cycles or hot flashes
  • Cravings for sugar or carbs
  • Difficulty falling or staying asleep

If several of these sound familiar, your hormone levels could be sending mixed signals.

One key clue: weight gain that resists diet and exercise. When you can’t seem to lose weight despite doing “everything right,” it’s often your hormones – especially, but not always, in postmenopausal women.

Recognizing these early patterns of persistent weight issues allows you to seek help before they spiral into long-term hormonal imbalance and metabolic fatigue.

But what role does fat play in the hormone connection?

How Does Fat Affect Hormones?

This is a two-way process: Hormonal imbalance influences body fat, and body fat influences hormone imbalance.

That’s because fat tissue is metabolically active. It releases signaling molecules that affect appetite, inflammation, and reproductive health.

One key messenger is leptin. When leptin levels rise, your brain should register that you’re full. But with excess body fat, those signals get muffled – a state called “leptin resistance.” Your brain thinks you’re starving, even when energy stores are high – which drives more eating and slows your metabolism.

Excess fat also alters hormone levels by producing small amounts of estrogen and reducing growth hormone, both of which change how your body stores and burns calories.

This is a vicious cycle! More fat means more hormonal disruption, and more disruption makes it harder to shed fat.

Breaking that fat loop through medical weight loss support will help your hormones find balance again.

Diabetes often develops quietly. You might feel fine, yet your body could already be showing early signs of imbalance in your blood sugar. That’s why screening for diabetes – through simple blood sugar checks – is such an important part of your annual health care.

A quick note, though: When we talk about diabetes in this article, we’re focusing on prediabetes and Type 2 diabetes. These are the most common forms of diabetes in the news these days, and they tend to develop over time.

What are Screening Tests for Breast Cancer?

Screening tests for breast cancer are medical exams designed to detect breast cancer before you have symptoms. They can find abnormalities at an early stage, when treatment is more effective and survival rates are higher.

There are three main categories of screening tests:

Imaging tests such as mammography, breast ultrasound, and breast MRI.

What are the Three Main Imaging Tests for Breast Cancer?

The three most common breast cancer screening tests are the mammogram, breast ultrasound, and breast MRI.

What are the Different Types of Mammograms?

There are three main types of screening mammography available today:

Traditional (2D) mammography

This older method captures two flat X-ray images of each breast. Many clinics still use it, but it’s gradually being replaced by newer technology.

Digital mammography

This is a more advanced approach where the images are stored electronically. Digital files allow radiologists to zoom in, adjust contrast, and share results more easily. This improves breast cancer screening accuracy.

3D mammography (tomosynthesis)

This technique takes multiple images from different angles, creating a layered view of breast tissue. It’s particularly helpful for women with dense breast tissue, because overlapping structures can make it harder to spot small tumors with 2D images.

Breast Ultrasound: An Alternative or Complement to Mammograms

A breast ultrasound is an imaging test that uses sound waves to create pictures of the inside of your breast. It’s not a replacement for a mammogram, but it can be an important complement in breast cancer screening.

If we compare having a mammogram vs. an ultrasound, mammograms are generally better at finding very small calcifications.

This technique uses a small amount of radioactive tracer to highlight cancerous cells. MBI may help detect breast cancer that traditional imaging misses, though availability is still limited.

Automated breast ultrasounds (ABUS)

These tests scan your entire breast with automated equipment, again making them more efficient for women with dense breast tissue than traditional ultrasound alone.

What is Hormone Replacement Therapy (HRT) for Men and Women

Hormone replacement therapy (HRT) can involve estrogen, progesterone, or testosterone, depending on whether you’re a woman or a man.

Hormones are chemical messengers that influence every system in your body.

For women, the most important female hormones are estrogen and progesterone, while men rely primarily on testosterone. Together, these hormones regulate reproduction, metabolism, bone health, brain function, and even cardiovascular health.

Hormones can get out of sync at any age. We call this “hormone imbalance” and advise hormone therapy where appropriate.

Hormone Replacement Therapy for Women

Hormone therapy is mostly used to treat menopause symptoms such as hot flashes, sleep disturbances, and mood swings. By addressing these issues, hormone replacement therapy HRT helps you regain quality of life during this natural stage of aging.

Women facing early menopause also benefit from starting hormone therapy sooner, since the loss of estrogen can affect bone health, heart health, and energy levels.

Hormone Replacement Therapy for Men

Hormone therapy for men is most often focused on testosterone. Age-related hormone decline, sometimes called andropause, can lead to fatigue, decreased muscle strength, and reduced sexual health. Hormone replacement helps restore your vitality, motivation, and balance.

In both men and women, the goal of replacement therapy is not just to relieve uncomfortable symptoms, but also to support long-term wellbeing.

Symptoms of Hormonal Imbalance in Women

During perimenopause, levels of female hormones estrogen and progesterone fall sharply, causing a range of common menopause symptoms such as hot flashes, night sweats, and mood fluctuations. Some women also notice weight changes, lower energy, low libido, and disrupted sleep.

The phrase “early menopause” simply implies these changes occur sooner than usual.

These annoying but common menopause symptoms can lead you to explore menopausal hormone therapy to restore balance and wellness.

Symptoms of Hormonal Imbalance in Men

Men’s gradual drop in testosterone (andropause) brings on symptoms of hormone imbalance in men that can include fatigue, decreased muscle mass, mood changes, and reduced libido.

Left untreated, low testosterone may also contribute to long-term concerns such as heart disease and bone loss.

For many, hormone replacement therapy for men provides an effective solution.

Hormone Therapy to Restore Balance

When levels of estrogen, progesterone, or testosterone are out of sync, the cluster of uncomfortable symptoms diminishes your quality of life.

Hormone therapy restores your natural balance, reduces symptoms, and helps you feel yourself again.

Imbalanced hormones, however, ultimately affect your overall health. So, hormone therapy for men and hormone replacement therapy for women also encourage healthy longevity.

But what types of hormone therapy are available?

Types of Hormone Replacement Therapy

There’s no single approach to hormone therapy. Different patients require different combinations, doses, and delivery methods.

Understanding the options helps you and your healthcare professional choose the plan that best addresses your needs.

Estrogen-Only Hormone Therapy

If you’ve had a hysterectomy, estrogen therapy is likely appropriate.

This type of hormone therapy is sometimes called estrogen-only HRT. Estrogen therapy can effectively relieve hot flashes, protect bone density, and improve vaginal health without the need for progesterone.

Combination Therapy

If you still have a uterus, you typically get combined HRT. This includes both estrogen and progesterone. These work together to treat menopausal symptoms while reducing the risk of complications such as endometrial cancer.

Combination hormone replacement therapy HRT is widely considered the standard approach for managing common menopause symptoms in women.

Testosterone Replacement Therapy for Men

The most common form of hormone replacement therapy for men is testosterone replacement therapy (TRT). Low testosterone can affect men at any age.

This type of hormone replacement addresses low testosterone levels and helps restore vitality, muscle strength, and sexual health.

Local Delivery Methods

Not all hormone treatments are systemic. Some therapies are delivered locally. Options include creams, gels, injections, patches, and pellets, each with its own advantages.

For example, low-dose vaginal estrogen is often used to relieve dryness, discomfort, and bladder issues without significantly affecting the rest of your body.

These different deliveries of hormone therapy allow for flexibility. Discuss the various options with your healthcare professional in light of your lifestyle and preferences.

Bioidentical Hormone Replacement Therapy (BHRT) – A Natural Option

In recent years, many patients have turned to bioidentical hormone replacement therapy (BHRT) as an alternative to traditional forms of hormone therapy.

These treatments use compounds that are chemically identical to the hormones produced naturally in the body, which is why they’re often called bioidentical hormones.

Customized Care for Each Patient

A key advantage of BHRT is personalization. Unlike a one-size-fits-all approach, replacement therapy with bioidentical hormones can be customized to match your unique needs.

This makes it possible for healthcare professionals to adjust doses and delivery methods more precisely, creating a treatment plan that feels more natural and balanced.

Benefits of BHRT for Women

BHRT for women provides relief from menopausal symptoms with formulations that are primarily estrogen or a combination of estrogen and progesterone, as we said. But they’re compounded in unique combinations for you.

This can be especially helpful if you undergo early menopause and are concerned about long-term bone and heart health. Many women view BHRT as a safer option here, offering symptom relief while minimizing risks.

Benefits of BHRT for Men

BHRT for men provides support for testosterone levels that decline with age.

Using bioidentical hormones, men can naturally restore energy, motivation, and overall vitality. This type of hormone replacement is often appealing for those who prefer a more natural approach to addressing low testosterone.

Why Patients Choose BHRT

Ultimately, the choice to pursue BHRT comes down to preference. You may simply prefer using treatments that are closer to your body’s own chemistry. Bioidentical options can offer effective relief while addressing your unique concerns and goals.

Benefits of Hormone Replacement Therapy for Women

For women, the main advantage of hormone replacement is relief from menopausal symptoms. Hot flashes, sleep problems, and mood swings – some of the most common menopause symptoms – can be greatly reduced with hormone therapy.

Depending on your history, personal risk factors, and whether you still have a uterus, your healthcare professional may prescribe estrogen therapy alone or in the form of combined HRT (estrogen plus progesterone). This can relieve hot flashes, protect bone density, and improve your vaginal health without the need for progesterone.

If you’ve undergone hysterectomy, you would need estrogen-only HRT (estrogen without progesterone) and have fewer side effects.

In either case, treatment is primarily estrogen, because estrogen is the hormone most responsible for benefiting you with a regulated body temperature, good bone density, and vaginal health.

Local symptoms of dryness or discomfort during intimacy can benefit from low-dose vaginal estrogen – which also targets improved bladder health.

The long-term benefits of hormone therapy for women – whether general hormone therapy or low-dose vaginal estrogen – are also important. Stronger bones, better sleep, improved cardiovascular health, and revitalized intimacy post menopause all contribute to your overall quality of life.

Benefits of Hormone Replacement Therapy for Men

Hormone therapy for men is primarily focused on testosterone.

A well-monitored plan of replacement therapy can increase your energy, rebuild muscle mass, improve your sexual health, and lift your mood. For many, men this also means improved motivation and sharper mental clarity.

Monitoring and Follow-Up of Hormone Therapy Treatment

For successful hormone replacement therapy (HRT) you should have an ongoing partnership with your healthcare provider or trained healthcare professional to monitor and adjust dosage.

Initial evaluation

Before beginning hormone therapy, you have lab tests to assess hormone levels and overall health. This helps determine whether hormone therapy is the best option and which type of treatment is appropriate for you.

Customized plans

Your healthcare provider will create a plan that reflects women’s or men’s unique health needs and goals. Women may need estrogen therapy, combined HRT, testosterone occasionally, or localized options such as low-dose vaginal estrogen. Men usually want testosterone support.

Regular check-ups and adjustments

Monitoring is key to safety. Regular follow-up visits and lab work allow healthcare professionals to track your hormone levels over the months, adjust doses, and minimize risks.

This delivers the benefits of hormone therapy effectively while protecting your long-term health.

Risks and Side Effects of Hormone Therapy

Like all medical treatments, hormone replacement therapy (HRT) has potential risks as well as benefits. Healthcare professionals don’t assume HRT fits all men and women.

The key is to weigh these factors carefully with your healthcare provider so that your plan is safe and effective for you.

Shared Risks and Side Effects of Hormone Therapy

Both men and women may experience temporary side effects when starting hormone therapy, such as bloating, mood shifts, or changes in weight. These issues often resolve once hormone levels stabilize and dosages are fine-tuned by healthcare professionals.

Hormone Therapy Risks for Women

For women, the risks of menopausal hormone therapy or other forms of replacement therapy may include blood clots, stroke, heart disease, and – depending on age and history – an increased risk of perhaps ovarian cancer or colorectal cancer.

The type of therapy also matters. For example, long-term estrogen therapy without progesterone is generally only used with women who’ve had a hysterectomy.

Those with personal risk factors, such as a strong family history of breast cancer or heart disease, may need closer monitoring by healthcare professionals, or alternative approaches.

Hormone Therapy Risks for Men

The risks of testosterone hormone replacement therapy for men typically include acne and sleep apnea – and, sometimes, changes in red blood cell count and possible infertility due to overstimulation.

In rare cases, testosterone therapy can affect cardiovascular health or prostate health.

Regular monitoring with a qualified healthcare provider ensures that the benefits of hormone replacement outweigh any potential complications.

Vaccines in pregnancy play an important role in protecting both you and your baby:

  • During pregnancy, your immune system works differently, which can make you more vulnerable to certain illnesses.
  • And vaccines you receive now can pass protective antibodies to your baby, giving them a healthier start in life.

So, what vaccines in pregnancy are important to get – and when should you have them?

Let’s walk through the key ones your healthcare provider will discuss with you, and why they matter.

What Vaccines Do Pregnant Women Get?

Doctors don’t recommend all vaccines in pregnancy, but a few are especially important. The top three are:

  • Flu vaccine – to protect both you and your newborn during flu season.
  • Tdap vaccine – to guard your baby against whooping cough, which can be very dangerous in the first months of life.
  • RSV vaccine – a newer option to protect infants from severe lung infections.

Your provider may also check your vaccine history. For example, if you haven’t had a COVID-19 booster or need hepatitis B protection, those may be discussed.

Some vaccines, like MMR (measles, mumps, rubella) or chickenpox, are not given during pregnancy but may be recommended before or after.

Let’s now look in turn at each of these major vaccines you should get in pregnancy.

Flu Vaccination for Pregnant Women

The flu is an inconvenience during pregnancy, for sure. You’re already undergoing many changes in your body! But a dose of flu can also lead to serious illness and complications. That’s why doctors recommend flu vaccination for pregnant women in any trimester.

Ideally, you should get your flu shot before flu season begins. But it’s safe to receive it later as well.

The bonus? Antibodies from the vaccine cross your placenta and protect your baby for the first six months of life. This is an age when they’re too young to get their own flu shot.

And to clear up a common worry: the flu vaccine cannot give you the flu. It’s “dead.” So it’s one of the simplest steps to keep both you and your baby safe.

Should I Get the Tdap Vaccine While Pregnant?

Yes. Tdap stands for tetanus, diphtheria, and pertussis (whooping cough). The Tdap vaccine while pregnant is strongly recommended during every pregnancy.

The main reason doctors give it in pregnancy is to protect your newborn from pertussis, which can cause severe breathing problems in babies.

Doctors recommend getting the Tdap vaccine between 27 and 36 weeks of pregnancy. This timing ensures your body creates strong antibodies and passes them to your baby before birth.

“But I’ve already had a Tdap shot. Why have another?”

We get asked that a lot. The answer is that even if you’ve had the Tdap vaccine in the past, you’ll still need it in each pregnancy, because antibody protection decreases over time.

When to Get RSV Vaccine if Pregnant

RSV (respiratory syncytial virus) is one of the leading causes of hospitalization in infants. For many families, it shows up like a bad cold, but for babies under six months, it can cause serious lung infections.

The good news is that there’s now a safe and effective RSV vaccine for pregnant women.

Here’s some proof – because we know pregnant women are often nervous about vaccines in pregnancy.

During the 2024–25 RSV season – when the maternal RSV vaccine and infant antibody treatment first became widely available in the US – infant hospitalizations dropped by over a quarter. And for babies under three months old, by as much as 45% to 52%.

So, getting the RSV vaccine is a really good move.

If you’re searching online for “when to get RSV vaccine pregnant,” the recommendation is usually between 32 and 36 weeks of pregnancy – and only during the RSV season (typically September through January).

This timing helps maximize the antibodies passed to your baby right before birth.

Your provider will let you know if this vaccine is right for you and how it fits with your other prenatal care.

Other Vaccines to Ask About When Pregnant

While flu, Tdap, and RSV are the main vaccines in pregnancy, your doctor may also review other options such as:

  • COVID-19 booster. This is safe and effective at any stage of pregnancy.
  • Hepatitis B. If you’re not already protected and are at risk of exposure, this is important.
  • MMR and varicella (chickenpox). These vaccines are not safe during pregnancy, but your provider may recommend them before or after.

Every pregnancy is unique, so the exact list of recommended vaccines may vary.

How to Talk with Your Provider About Vaccines in Pregnancy

It’s normal to have questions about vaccines, especially when you’re pregnant and feel more vulnerable. The best step is an open conversation with your Obgyn or primary care provider.

Here are a few helpful questions to ask:

  • Which vaccines are recommended for me in this trimester?
  • Can I get them here at the clinic or should I schedule separately?
  • What side effects should I expect?
  • How do these vaccines help protect my baby after birth?

Remember: Timing is everything. Doctors schedule vaccines at specific weeks during your pregnancy to give you and your baby the strongest possible protection at the right moment.

Quick Summary of Vaccinations During Pregnancy

Vaccines in pregnancy are about double protection – safeguarding your health while giving your baby a strong start. Here’s a recap of the three most important ones to know about:

  • Flu vaccine – safe in any trimester, vital for flu season
  • Tdap vaccine – recommended in every pregnancy, protects against whooping cough
  • RSV vaccine – available in late pregnancy during RSV season to prevent newborn illness

Other vaccines may also be discussed, depending on your medical history.
