How to lose weight with PCOS (the usual way we refer to polycystic ovarian syndrome) is often challenging, but it’s definitely not impossible.

If you have a PCOS diagnosis, you’ve come to the right place to learn about how to lose weight with PCOS.

But first we need to briefly clarify two things:

The first is that you can have multiple ovarian cysts but not have PCOS. There’s a difference. And as we mentioned in our blog on ovarian cysts and birth control pills, they may cause bloating but not usually weight gain – although it can feel like it.

The second is that multiple ovarian cysts might be linked to PCOS sometimes. A polycystic ovarian syndrome specialist will be able to tell you how PCOS is diagnosed, how to get tested for PCOS, and offer a specific PCOS test – such as the PCOS ultrasound test.

But here, we’re going to look at how to lose weight with PCOS. Why is it so hard?

Why Weight Loss With PCOS Can Be Hard

Weight loss with PCOS is affected by hormonal and metabolic factors that don’t apply in the same way to everyone else.

One of the most significant is insulin resistance, which is common in PCOS. When insulin levels are consistently high, the body is more likely to store fat and less likely to access stored fat for energy.

PCOS can also affect how your body regulates appetite and energy use.

Hormonal imbalances may increase hunger signals, reduce satiety after meals, and make blood sugar swings more pronounced.

This is why standard calorie-cutting approaches often lead to short-term results followed by plateaus or rapid regain.

Another issue is this: In PCOS, your body may burn fewer calories at rest than expected, particularly during aggressive dieting. That’s why restrictive diets are often ineffective over time if you have PCOS.

None of this means weight loss with PCOS isn’t achievable. You just need an effective strategy. One that reduces insulin resistance and stress levels over time.

How to Lose Weight With PCOS: Targeting Polycystic Ovarian Syndrome Belly

You may have noticed that your extra weight gain is mostly around your abdomen. This pattern is closely linked to insulin resistance and cortisol (often referred to as the stress hormone).

When insulin levels remain elevated, fat storage is more likely to occur centrally than in your hips or thighs. Stress hormones can amplify this effect. Particularly if your sleep is poor or exercise intensity is too high for recovery.

This pattern can be frustrating.

But belly fat is responsive if you can improve your insulin sensitivity. For example, with resistance training, balanced meals with adequate protein and fiber, and consistent meal timing.

Targeting abdominal fat by addressing blood sugar regulation, stress load, and muscle preservation is what will bring you gradual but measurable change.

What Is the PCOS Diet?

There isn’t a single prescribed PCOS diet. But there are clear nutritional patterns that support PCOS weight loss by improving blood sugar control and reducing insulin spikes. You’re aiming for stability.

Foods That Support PCOS Weight Loss

Meals that help you lose weight with PCOS tend to combine fiber, protein, and healthy fats. This slows digestion, reduces blood sugar swings, and helps control your appetite across the day. It also helps your gut health.

Fiber-rich carbohydrates are a key part of this in appropriate portions.

Whole grains such as oatmeal, brown rice, and quinoa, along with beans, lentils, vegetables, and fruit, help regulate insulin response.

Protein is equally important.

Include protein at every meal – from foods such as fish, eggs, poultry, legumes, tofu, or Greek yogurt. This supports muscle mass and helps you feel full for longer.

Maintaining muscle is particularly important for PCOS weight loss. Why? Because muscle tissue improves insulin sensitivity.

Healthy fats also play a role.

Foods such as olive oil, nuts, seeds, and avocado support hormone function and slow absorption of carbs when eaten together in a meal.

Overall, diets that are plant-forward, minimally processed, and balanced tend to be easier to maintain and more effective for PCOS weight loss than highly restrictive plans.

You can start swapping these foods in gradually to make the change easier. And also swap some foods out…

Foods to Limit if You’re Trying to Lose Weight With PCOS

Foods that cause rapid blood sugar spikes can make weight loss with PCOS more difficult. These include refined carbs, sugary drinks, sweets, and heavily processed snack foods.

Highly processed foods are often low in fiber and high in added sugars or refined starches. This can increase hunger shortly after eating.

Regularly relying on these foods can undermine your weight-loss efforts even when you’ve monitored calories.

Limiting these foods doesn’t mean complete avoidance. The focus is frequency and portion size, not perfection.

Meal Timing and Balance Helps PCOS Weight Loss

Skipping meals or eating erratically can worsen blood sugar instability. Regular meals with a balance of carbohydrates, protein, and fat tend to support more consistent energy levels and appetite control.

What Else Helps You Lose Weight With PCOS?

Food choices form the foundation of PCOS weight loss, but lifestyle factors influence how effectively those changes work. Here are two to bear in mind:

Exercise Can Support PCOS Weight Loss

Exercise is most effective for PCOS when it improves insulin sensitivity rather than simply burning calories.

Resistance training plays a central role here. Building and maintaining muscle helps your body use glucose more efficiently and supports metabolic health over time.

Consistent low-impact cardio such as walking, cycling, or swimming can also be helpful. These support fat loss without placing excessive stress on your body.

However, very high volumes of intense cardio are not always helpful for PCOS. In some cases, they can increase stress hormones and make appetite and fatigue harder to manage.

Sleep and Stress Management

Poor sleep and chronic stress can interfere with PCOS weight loss by increasing cortisol levels and disrupting appetite regulation.

Even small improvements in sleep duration or consistency can support better blood sugar control and reduce your cravings.

And finding ways to manage your stress levels can help support your weight loss efforts.

You can get pretty fed up when the pounds pile on – even though you’re eating healthily, staying active, and doing your best to take care of yourself. We get it. So – what else can be the culprit? Can hormones cause weight gain like this?

Yes, they can.

Hormones and weight gain are closely linked because hormones control the messages your body sends – about hunger, fat storage, energy use, and even sleep.

When they fall out of balance, those signals can get scrambled, and your body starts to hold on to extra weight no matter what you do.

The good news is that when you restore the balance in your hormone system, your body usually begins to cooperate again.

So let’s look at what’s happening and how to solve it.

Can Hormones Cause Weight Gain? Here’s How They Affect Your Body

As we mentioned, your hormones are your body’s communication system. They tell your cells when to burn calories, when to store fat, and when to rest.

When this system works smoothly, your metabolism works well too.

Hormonal changes can happen to both men and women.

But when individual hormones get out of sync for any reason – maybe due to pregnancy, perimenopause, menopause, thyroid changes, or long-term stress – the messages don’t transmit properly.

That’s why you often find hormone imbalance and weight gain together. For example:

  • Estrogen and progesterone influence where fat is stored. When estrogen drops, weight may collect around the abdomen.
  • Thyroid hormones set your metabolic speed. Low levels can slow your body’s calorie-burning ability.
  • Cortisol, your stress hormone, raises blood sugar and can increase cravings.
  • Insulin helps control blood sugar, but high levels can push your body toward fat storage.
  • Testosterone helps maintain muscle. Less of it can mean less calorie-burning muscle mass.

So you can stop feeling guilty if you’re doing everything else right. Hormone problems and weight gain are more to do with your biology than pure willpower.

And now you know that, you can start thinking about how to get your hormones back in balance! But first, here’s another question we get asked…

Can Hormones Cause Weight Gain and Fatigue at the Same Time?

Yes. The two often go hand in hand. When hormones such as thyroid, cortisol, or insulin drift out of balance, your energy levels take a hit. You may wake up tired, rely on caffeine to push through the day, and feel too exhausted to exercise in the evening.

This is why so many people ask, “Can hormones cause weight gain and fatigue?” When your metabolism slows and your energy drops, daily movement declines and you don’t use calories as efficiently.

You find yourself in a frustrating cycle: Fatigue limits your activity, and inactivity fuels further weight gain.

If that sounds familiar, remember you’re not lazy. Your hormones are sending mixed signals. But with proper medical guidance, you can restore both balance and energy.

Can improving gut health help you lose weight? Yes! Good gut health and weight loss are connected in ways that are not always obvious.

For example, gut inflammation can hinder weight loss. Inflammation stems from imbalances in your gut microbiome (the community of bacteria, fungi, and viruses that keeps your body processes in sync).

Key players in preventing gut inflammation are balanced hormones, vitamins, and eating healthy whole food as often as possible to feed the various beneficial bacteria in your gut.

If you keep your gut metabolism working well, your weight-loss journey will be easier.

But you don’t have to solve this alone. Your primary care physician for weight loss is the ideal person to help you sustain your efforts until you reach your target weight.

For example, they may:

  • Diagnose any digestive issues you have
  • Recommend some dietary changes to encourage gut health and weight loss
  • Review your hormone levels to avoid imbalances that can inflame your gut
  • Support you with practical steps to achieve your long-term goals

Digestive Issues that Hinder Weight Loss

Your gut will gradually digest your food properly when it’s healthy. But are you wondering how to tell if your gut is healthy? These are some signs your gut may be struggling:

  • Constipation
  • Bloating
  • Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS)
  • Diarrhea
  • Heartburn
  • Cramps
  • Tiredness
  • Food intolerance

Your primary care doctor can help you diagnose any of these digestive issues and provide treatment where necessary.

But let’s look at some dietary changes you can start today to improve your gut health and help you on your weight loss journey.

Dietary Changes to Encourage Good Gut Health and Weight Loss

Your gut operates a complex bio-system, acts as your “second brain,” and coordinates your overall health and immunity.

Many things can disrupt this system. These include lifestyle choices (adequate sleep and regular activity are essential), stress, and illness.

But here are some dietary changes and additions your primary care provider may advise you about to encourage sustainable weight loss:

Vitamins for Gut Health and Weight Loss

Vitamin B12 produces stomach acid to break down food effectively. B12 is found in meat, fish, eggs, and dairy. If you’re vegetarian or vegan, a B12 supplement is fine.

Vitamin C helps reduce inflammation and can help heal the gut lining in your stomach and intestines. Find it in oranges, strawberries, broccoli, Brussels sprouts, etc.

Vitamin D is a natural anti-inflammatory and helps improve your gut microbiome. You can find vitamin D in oily fish, red meat, and egg yolks. Again, your primary care provider can help you choose a supplement or fortified food if you don’t eat meat and dairy.

Whole Foods vs. Ultra-Processed Foods (UPFs)

The press has been full of articles about UPFs recently. These are foods specifically ultra-processed to enhance taste and the feeling of wanting more – as well as to improve shelf life!

Many of these UPFs disrupt your gut health. There are two problems:

  • Processing food can damage the natural nutrients in the food
  • Preservatives to improve shelf life are not usually natural. You won’t find them in your store cupboard!

So, if you want to encourage gut health and weight loss, choose whole foods because they’re on your side nutritionally. They’re less disruptive to your gut.

However, your weight loss journey is a process not an overnight achievement! Try swapping UPFs out gradually to make the changes easier.

Tip: Whole foods to look out for include fresh fruit and veg, whole grains, nuts, seeds, beans, herbs, and spices. Minimally processed meat is also fine, but target unprocessed plant foods where possible.

We often get asked about adding in a probiotic supplement. Let’s look at that next.

Best Probiotic for Gut Health and Weight Loss

Probiotics are live bacteria and also yeasts – they live in your gut naturally.

They help you absorb the vitamin C we mentioned earlier and also have a beneficial impact on your metabolism – fighting off the less friendly type of bacteria in your gut!

Since gut health and weight loss are intrinsically linked, as we’ve mentioned, here are the two best probiotics to take if your healthcare provider recommends them:

  • Lactobacillus species
  • Bifidobacterium genus

Find a reputable brand and check the type of live culture in it. Your primary care provider can help you decide.

Tip: Good ones may include, for example, Lactobacillus Plantarum, Casei, and Paracasei, along with Bifidobacterium Breve, Lactis, and Bifidum.

You can also find these probiotics naturally in fermented foods. See below.

Best Foods for Gut Health and Weight Loss

We’ve mentioned whole foods and their benefit. For other foods that help your gut and encourage weight loss, consider ones high in fiber, and also some fermented ones.

High-fiber foods

These help you feel full for longer and are often lower in calories – so that’s a bonus if you’re trying to lose weight.

But they also help food travel through your gut, bulking out your stool and avoiding constipation (one of the signs of an unhealthy gut that we mentioned earlier).

Tip: Fruits, vegetables, legumes and whole grains come with lots of fiber.

Fermented foods

These have beneficial bacteria to keep your microbiome balanced. Be careful to avoid those that have added sugar and flavorings or you lose the benefit. You can always add berries if you dislike their plain taste.

Tip: Yogurt, kefir, and kimchi are fermented.

Balanced Hormone Levels to Maintain Good Gut Metabolism

Your gut orchestrates your hormones. And be aware that imbalance can strike at any age. So, check out your hormone levels if you struggle to maintain weight loss. Imbalances in insulin, cortisol, or thyroid levels, for example, can affect your weight.

Tip: At Stella Mattina, we recommend BioTe pellets to rebalance hormones when necessary.

Practical Steps Towards Gut Health and Weight Loss Goals

Small steps have an immediate overall effect on gut health. Choose one of our tips and do just that before adding a second:

1 Eliminate sugar from your hot or cold drinks.

2 Eat a handful of almonds daily.

3 Try Greek yogurt with blueberries.

4 Cook one meal with no processed ingredients.

5 Add some fiber-rich black or pinto beans to a salad.
